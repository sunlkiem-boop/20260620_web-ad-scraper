# Research Framework

Distilled from the user's "Research Part 1" and "Research Part 2" working framework. This is the question architecture you answer through web research before writing the VOC Dossier.

The framework has **5 layers of inquiry**. Hit all 5 with depth, not breadth. Use this as a checklist while you search.

---

## Layer 1 — Demographic & psychographic

Answer these questions for your audience. The output goes into VOC §2 (persona snapshots) and §6 (trigger event timeline).

1. **Who is the customer?** Age band, gender skew, location, household income range, profession, education. Concrete numbers — "55+ male middle income US," not "people who care about health."
2. **Attitudes** — religious, political, social, economic. The audience may not be religious / political at all — note when that's the answer. Useful for understanding what voices land vs. repel.
3. **Hopes and dreams** — what they want OUT of their life, not just out of the product. Retirement at 55? Run a marathon at 60? Stay strong for grandkids? Be the friend who "has the answer"? Capture both the small and the cosmic.
4. **Victories and failures** — what's worked for them so far in this pain-point category, and what hasn't. The failure stories are the gold here.
5. **Outside forces they blame for preventing their best life** — "Wall Street is rigged," "Big Pharma is crooked," "Influencers ruined wellness." These narratives are what they tell themselves and what you can choose to validate (or not).
6. **Prejudices** — not race/gender — *category* prejudices. "Premier Protein customers are people who don't read labels." "Influencer brands are all marketing tax." "Cottage cheese is for 1990s diet women." Useful for in-group / out-group copy moves.
7. **Core beliefs about life, love, family** — sum them up in 1–3 sentences per persona. This is the "if I were writing a letter to one customer, who am I writing to" anchor.

---

## Layer 2 — Existing solutions (what the market is currently using)

Answer these for the dominant 5–10 incumbent products. Output goes into VOC §3 (pain points), §5 (competitive teardown), and §6 (timeline).

1. **What is the market using right now?** List all major competitors, including indirect ones. For protein RTDs: Premier, Fairlife, Owyn, Iconic, Muscle Milk Pro, Orgain, Quest, Slate, Isopure, Kirkland, Garden of Life. For supplements: AG1, Ritual, Centrum, Thorne, Bloom. For meal-replacement: Soylent, Huel, Ka'Chava.
2. **What's their experience been like with the existing solutions?** A lot? A little? Did they stop using them? Why?
3. **What does the market like about existing solutions?** Fast and easy? Cheap at Costco? Tastes like chocolate milk? Real ingredients? Influencer-recommended? This is the BAR your product must clear — and the validation language you can echo.
4. **What does the market DISLIKE about existing solutions?** Sucralose aftertaste? Carrageenan? Texture? Price? Subscription friction? Bloating? Plant-protein taste? These are the WEDGES your product can use.
5. **Are there horror stories about existing solutions?** Worst-case experiences. Class actions, recall events, "I drank this for a year and my UC flared." These make the most compelling proof in long-form copy.
6. **Does the market believe existing solutions work?** This is where your audience's "stage of sophistication" comes from. If they believe protein shakes work (yes — protein-shake category is Sophistication Level 4–5), you have to differentiate on mechanism. If they're skeptical of the whole category (e.g. NMN supplementation), you have to introduce the category first.

## Layer 3 — Curiosity / lost-discovery

This is the **lost-history layer**. People love "what's old is new again." Find at least one rich story for your category.

Examples by category:

- **Protein RTD:** Cosmonaut tube rations (1961 Vostok-1), Metrecal (1959 Mead Johnson invention), Carnation Instant Breakfast pivoting from breakfast to weight-loss, Sustagen as the hospital-food precursor.
- **Multivitamins / supplements:** Linus Pauling and vitamin C, Vitamin B12 winning the 1934 Nobel, the 1941 RDA project, the methylated B12 vs. cyanocobalamin distinction.
- **Pre-workout:** Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 1990, BCAAs from the body-building niche to mainstream, 1980s coffee-as-pre-workout.
- **Hormonal / women's wellness:** ancient menstrual rituals, Margaret Sanger and the Pill, the WHI study that scared a generation off HRT in 2002.

The story must be **factually true**. The editorial frame ("the FDA buried this for 30 years," "this won the war but got forgotten") is yours — but the underlying facts must hold.

What the lost-discovery does: it gives your VSL / long-form ad an anchor that flatters the customer's intelligence ("you'll be one of the few who knows this"). It's catnip for the Avatar 1 / label-reader profile.

---

## Layer 4 — Corruption / Fall-from-Eden

This is the **Why-was-our-paradise-stolen-from-us** layer. The premise: the customer's pain didn't always exist, or didn't always exist this badly, and an outside force is responsible. Find at least one rich story for your category.

Examples by category:

- **Protein RTD:** Sucralose's accidental 1976 invention + 1998 FDA approval after McNeil asked the FDA to withhold a diabetic-blood-sugar study; carrageenan's 1961 FDA-GRAS classification + the food-grade vs. degraded distinction that the FDA has refused to clarify; the protein-content-claim FDA loophole that lets brands use 2g of one amino acid to call themselves "complete"; ultra-processing creep documented by 2024 Lancet diabetes study.
- **Multivitamins:** Pfizer/Centrum's magnesium oxide (4% absorption) chosen over magnesium glycinate (60%) because oxide is cheaper; cyanocobalamin (synthetic B12 with a cyanide molecule) chosen over methylcobalamin because cyanocobalamin patents expired earlier; the proprietary-blend FDA loophole.
- **Pre-workout:** DMAA's 2013 FDA action, the 200mg/serving caffeine ceiling and how it gets stacked, the synephrine workaround after ephedra was banned.
- **Diet industry:** Ancel Keys's 1948 anti-fat campaign, the 1992 USDA food pyramid + grain industry lobbying, the protein-as-villain era from low-fat dogma.

What the Fall-from-Eden does: it gives the customer permission to be angry — and angry customers convert. It also positions your brand as the *restoration* of something lost, not the *introduction* of something new.

Avoid: conspiracy-coded framing that the FTC could action ("Big Pharma is hiding the cure"). Lean instead on **specific, documented, named** corporate / regulatory choices. Cite WHO did WHAT in WHAT year.

---

## Layer 5 — The wedge / mechanism arithmetic

For each pain point you've documented in Layers 2–4, capture the *mechanism* — the biological / regulatory / supply-chain reason it exists.

Examples for protein RTD:

- **The chalkiness mechanism** — pure whey isolate denatures during high-heat processing and ultrafiltration; the resulting protein particle aggregates, creating chalky texture. MPI (milk protein isolate) avoids this because it preserves the native casein matrix. *Story-grade:* "Whey concentrate → whey isolate → milk protein isolate" is a 60-year industrial-engineering journey that you can frame as the unsung evolution.
- **The bloating mechanism** — lactose + carrageenan + cellulose gum amplify each other; ultra-processed thickeners pass through the colon and disrupt the microbiome (Lancet 2024).
- **The "0g sugar" labeling mechanism** — FDA allows brands to claim "0g sugar" if they use zero-calorie artificial sweeteners. So Premier puts sucralose + Ace-K in the can and prints "1g sugar" on the front of the label. The customer doesn't know that "no sugar" means "lots of sucralose."
- **The plant-protein chalk mechanism** — pea protein lacks methionine and has a strong vegetal aftertaste; brands mask it with stevia, which has its own aftertaste; the two stack rather than cancel.

The mechanism arithmetic IS the UMS (unique mechanism of the solution) in the eventual Offer Brief. Capture it during research.

## Where to find the answers

The 7 research waves listed in `SKILL.md` (direct competitors → adjacent solutions → expert rankings → Reddit aggregators → curiosity → corruption → trigger events) map directly to Layers 1–5 above. Each wave should produce 25–40 quotes; total quota is 200+.

**Most-productive single sources** (these will repay 30 minutes of careful WebFetch'ing more than any cold web search):

- The competitor's own testimonial / reviews page on their site
- Amazon product page (sort by 5-star, then by 1-star — both equally valuable)
- Walmart product page (1-star reviews are particularly raw and verbatim)
- Reddit-quoting aggregators: `usefulvitamins.com`, `redditrecs.com`, `redrecs.com`, `rankedbyreddit.com`
- The dietitian roundup pages (Healthline / EatingWell / Verywell Fit / Today's Dietitian / Abbey Sharp)
- Trustpilot for the brand's own customer service
- Wikipedia for the lost-discovery and Fall-from-Eden facts
- ConsumerLab / Center for Science in the Public Interest / Consumer Reports for the corruption / safety angles
- Major newspaper coverage of recalls / class actions

**Sources to use sparingly:**
- The brand's own marketing copy (mine for credibility stack, never for customer voice)
- AI-generated review aggregators (often hallucinated; verify each quote against the original source)
- TikTok comments (use only with named-creator attribution + URL)

## Verbatim discipline

**Every quote** in the VOC dossier must:
1. Be attributed with a URL or named source (reviewer name + platform)
2. Be a real quote from a real public source (never invented)
3. Use real customer language — not a paraphrase that sounds like a customer
4. Be numbered sequentially in §3 so downstream documents can reference by number

**If you can't find a quote that supports a specific claim:**
- Do a top-up search (you missed it)
- Or flag the claim as "supported by composite pattern" (and reduce the claim)
- Or drop the claim

Never write "many customers say…" or "users often report…" — those phrasings tell the reader you didn't have the receipts.
